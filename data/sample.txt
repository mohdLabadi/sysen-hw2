
Foreign Exchange — Core Ideas

A foreign exchange rate tells you how much of one currency is needed to buy another. Quotes are usually written as a currency pair: the first currency is the base, and the second is the quote. For example, if EUR/USD is 1.08, one euro costs 1.08 U.S. dollars.

Retail and corporate users care about spot rates—the price for settlement “on the spot” (typically two business days for many currency pairs). Banks and brokers publish bid and ask prices: the bid is what they will pay to buy the base currency, and the ask is what they charge to sell it. The difference is the spread; a tight spread usually means a liquid market.

Exchange rates move because of interest rates, inflation expectations, growth outlooks, risk sentiment, and central-bank policy. When comparing rates over time, always note the quote convention (direct vs indirect) and whether you are looking at interbank mid rates or customer-facing prices.
